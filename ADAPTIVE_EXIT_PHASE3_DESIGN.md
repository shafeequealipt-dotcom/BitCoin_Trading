# Adaptive Exit — Phase 3 Design And Replay Evidence

This is the Phase 3 design deliverable required by IMPLEMENT_DYNAMIC_ADAPTIVE_EXIT_SYSTEM.md: the per-trade dynamic adaptive exit design, presented with a replay against the real logged trades, for operator approval before any implementation. Nothing has been implemented; the only code written is the read-only replay harness simulate_adaptive_exit_replay.py. Prose with heading structure for screen-reader access: no tables, no emoji, no decorative separators.

## Part 1 — The core design

The whole design rests on one number per coin, R, the Average True Range as a percentage of price, read from the existing volatility profiler (src/analysis/volatility_profile.py, VolatilityProfiler.get_profile, the atr_pct_5m field) and its volatility class. Every exit threshold becomes a bounded multiple of R, and every profit threshold is floored at the trade's round-trip fee so a locked win is net-positive.

The fee floor is the spine of the design, not a sibling of the other fixes. Every profit threshold is the larger of its R-multiple and the fee floor, where the fee floor is the round-trip taker cost (0.11 percent, from cap_round_trip_fee_pct, drawn from one canonical constant) times a small safety buffer. The arm, the locks, and the take-profit are never set below the fee, so the system can only ever lock real net profit, never a gross figure fees erase.

The geometry, with the starting multiples the replay tuned (Part 4), each bounded by a hard floor and ceiling so an ATR spike or collapse cannot make it absurd:

The arm is the maximum of 0.5R and the fee floor. The ladder rungs are at 1.5R, 3R, and 5R, with staged capture: at 1.5R the stop moves to break-even-plus (the fee floor, a free roll); at 3R it secures roughly 1.5R of profit; at 5R it locks most and lets the remainder run on the trail. The trail sits a fraction of R behind the running peak, floored at the fee, and its minimum-distance floor is lowered so a small move can be trailed rather than clamped to breakeven. The replay shows that for the realized move sizes a trail of about 0.5R captures materially more than 1R, because these moves are only about one R in size; the trail multiple is therefore a tuned value near 0.5R, not a fixed 1R, and stays bounded. The take-profit is a reachable 4R to 6R rather than a flat 6 percent. The hard stop is a wide 8R to 10R below the catastrophic cap, never above it. The initial ATR stop already uses 3R and is aligned to the same R for consistency.

All thresholds and coefficients live in one new centralized configuration section read once at boot with a boot sentinel, so no new hardcoded value is introduced while the old ones are removed.

## Part 2 — Recompute, smoothing, and per-trade parallelism

R is recomputed as the trade lives, so a coin that wakes up gets wider geometry and a coin that goes quiet gets tighter geometry. R is read from the profiler, which already caches it on a roughly sixty-to-one-hundred-twenty-second time-to-live, so it is step-wise stable rather than tick-jittery. A short moving average is applied at the fetch boundary in the owner function (using the existing per-position tracking state) so the geometry breathes with the coin without vibrating with noise. The existing minimum-change throttle and the gateway maximum-step and rate-limit rules stay in place as the damping. The geometry computation is a set of pure functions of R, the fee, and the coefficients, with no state and no input or output, so two positions computing geometry never share state; each touches only its own stop. This is the non-colliding, per-trade, parallel shape the prompt requires, and it is what makes the replay possible offline.

## Part 3 — Integration (woven in, not a separate system)

The R-geometry functions are added to the existing src/analysis/vol_scale.py, which is already the pure-function home for volatility scaling and already feeds the gateway. The existing owner functions (the ladder in _compute_ladder_floor, the trail in _compute_trail_stop, the graduation latch, the initial ATR stop, the development guard) fetch R from the already-injected profiler and the fee from config, call these functions, and feed the results into their existing logic. Their function bodies do not change; only the numbers they compute with change. There is no new worker, no new service, no new trading gate.

The owner hierarchy is unchanged. The Head, the green owner, the red owner, and the advisory systems still decide who writes the stop; the adaptive layer only changes what value they write. The catastrophic cap stays sacred and only tightens. The behavioral gates of the loss engine (the age gate, the MAE-to-stop ratio, the monotonic-grind cut, the structural-invalidation gate, and the win-probability model) are left untouched; only the distance and room values, which are already partly ATR-scaled, are floored at the fee.

The gateway is made to stop clamping the adaptive locks away. The replay confirms on the real, unmodified gateway that when the armed profit lock is held at its R-derived value (passed as the held floor from a trusted profit source), it writes, whereas the current behaviour, which holds only at breakeven, is dropped as a clamp-noop. Tighten-only, the wrong-side guard, the maximum-step rule, the rate-limit, and the catastrophic precedence are all preserved exactly.

## Part 4 — The replay evidence

The replay (simulate_adaptive_exit_replay.py) reconstructs all twenty trades in the captured one-hour window from the logged truth: the real flat outcome from COORD_CLOSE_START, the realized path and the true peak from the sniper's own M4_DECISION pnl and peak_pnl, and R from the VOL_PROFILE atr_pct. It walks each trade's realized path, applies the R-based geometry with the realized give-back appended so the trail is tested honestly, and reports the outcome net of each trade's round-trip fee. It also drives the real gateway on the canonical clamp-noop trace.

The headline, net of the 0.11 percent round-trip fee on the twenty trades. The flat geometry won zero of twenty trades net of fee, for a net sum of minus 3.469 percent. The adaptive geometry at the best trail (0.5R) won six of twenty, for a net sum of minus 1.198 percent, an improvement of plus 2.271 percent over the twenty trades. The trail sweep, which is how the multiple is tuned: a 1R trail recovers plus 1.961 percent, a 0.75R trail plus 2.021 percent, and a 0.5R trail plus 2.271 percent, because the realized moves are only about one R in size, so a tighter trail captures more.

The decomposition is the honest core, and it is exactly the bridge to the entry question the operator asked the replay to report. Four of the twenty trades (HYPERUSDT, LINKUSDT, FILUSDT, APTUSDT) had a peak that never cleared their own round-trip fee; no exit geometry can make those net-positive, and they sum to minus 1.298 percent under both the flat and the adaptive geometry. The other sixteen trades, whose peak did clear the fee, go from minus 2.171 percent under the flat geometry to plus 0.100 percent under the adaptive geometry. In one sentence: the exit fix turns the sixteen normal trades from a 2.17 percent bleed into roughly break-even by stopping the give-back, and the entire residual loss is concentrated in the four trades whose moves never cleared cost, which is the entries' problem to solve, not the exit's.

The gateway validation, on the real SLGateway with the owner gate isolated: the flat lock held at breakeven was rejected as a clamp-noop, and the same lock held at its R-derived value was accepted. This confirms the single most delicate interaction works as designed.

## Part 5 — Honest note on the 70 percent target

The adaptive exit does what the prompt says it can: it stops the give-back and makes wins net-positive, a large and measurable gain proven on the real trades (the give-back trades go from minus 2.17 to plus 0.10 percent, and the net loss on the window is cut by about two thirds). It does not, on this window, reach a 70 percent win rate or a net-positive window by itself, and it must not be claimed to. The replay shows why, in numbers: the win rate net of fee rises from zero to thirty percent, and the residual loss is four trades whose moves never cleared their own fee plus several whose peak barely reached half an R. The remaining distance to the target is the entries' to close, by producing moves that clear cost; the R and fee-floor data this design produces measures that distance exactly, coin by coin. The adaptive geometry is the right and necessary foundation regardless, because an exit must be sized to each coin's volatility, and the replay proves it recovers the give-back that is the dominant, proven loss.

## Part 6 — Scope, per the operator's steers

The fee floor (master item M4) is the spine: every Tier-1 threshold floors at it, and the replay reports the fee-clearance count as the bridge to the entry question, as directed.

The uncertain behavioral-gate magnitudes (master item M12) are out of scope by default; the working protections are left alone. The replay does not show any specific behavioral-gate magnitude costing money on this window, so none is raised for change now. If a future replay or live evidence shows a specific magnitude costs money, it will be brought back as its own separate gated decision, never folded into the rescaling.

The validator and default-stop bounds (master item M15) are triaged: the clearly exit-geometry items are in scope (the recommend-path minimum-stop filter and the default stop loss percent, which set the protective stop distance), and the entry-side or ambiguous items (the order-construction validator sanity bounds, the structural proximity bands used at entry) are deferred. Narrower when in doubt.

## Part 7 — The two escalation decisions (presented with evidence, not pre-decided)

Escalation one, the gateway profit-lock exemption semantics. The replay proves the mechanism works on the existing carve-out when the R-lock is supplied as the held floor from a trusted source. The decision is how to wire it. Option A reuses the existing breakeven-floor parameter, passing the R-lock in place of the breakeven price; this is the smallest change, caller-side only, and is already proven by the replay, but it overloads the parameter's meaning and its log line still says breakeven. Option B adds an explicit profit-lock-floor parameter and its own log line, a small, auditable gateway change that keeps the breakeven and profit-lock semantics distinct; the codebase's own comment on the bypass allowlist says additions should be auditable, which favours Option B. The recommendation is Option B for auditability, but the choice is yours.

Escalation two, the dead-drifter age discriminator. The scratch-exit releases the development guard for a trade whose lifetime peak never reached 1R and that is old enough to be judged dead. The replay confirms the dead drifters in the window (LINKUSDT held 2,637 seconds, HYPERUSDT 1,574, APTUSDT 1,283) are among the four that never cleared their fee, so a scratch would chiefly free capital sooner rather than change their tiny net outcome; the per-trade profit-and-loss does not by itself pin the exact age, because the value of the scratch is capital efficiency and late-bloomer safety, not a price gain. The decision is the age fraction at which a no-1R trade is declared dead, and the requirement that the scratch never bypasses the existing late-bloomer veto. The recommendation is to set the discriminator conservatively (release only well past the minimum-hold and only when the existing signs-of-life veto is silent), but the exact fraction is yours to set.

## Part 8 — Ship sequence and verification

The sequence is unchanged from the approved plan and master list, gated by the fee floor and the gateway. Commit 0 lands the centralized configuration, the canonical fee constant, and the pure R-geometry functions in vol_scale.py, with a boot sentinel and no behavior change. Commit 1 ships the gateway profit-lock exemption (the option chosen at escalation one), log-only first then enforcing, verified by the gateway validation in the replay and a forced catastrophic-stop test. Commit 2 rescales the ladder, the trail (near 0.5R), and the take-profit and adopts the fee floor, shipped only after Commit 1 enforces, verified by re-running the replay and confirming the give-back recovery. Commit 3 R-derives the hard stop and the initial stop, always below the sacred cap, verified by the forced catastrophic-stop test. Commit 4 adds the dead-drifter scratch (the discriminator chosen at escalation two). Commit 5 verifies and aligns the faded-winner and recovery-capture paths. Each commit is atomic and individually revertible on main, with timestamped backups, its own boot sentinel and observable logs, and the protected-table counts re-checked. Verification follows the operator's standing ritual, sequential passes, never parallel.

## Part 9 — The verdict, stated plainly

The adaptive exit is the necessary and dominant fix for the proven loss: it stops the give-back, makes wins net-positive, and the replay proves it on the real trades, turning the sixteen fee-clearing trades from a 2.17 percent net bleed into roughly break-even and cutting the window's net loss by about two thirds, with the gateway clamp-noop resolved on the real gateway. It does not by itself reach the 70 percent target on this window, and the replay shows honestly that the remaining loss is concentrated in trades whose moves never cleared their own fee, which is the entries' question to answer next. This design awaits operator approval before any implementation begins.
